Your focus should move from penetration testing to intrusion detection / incident response

Dieter Vandenbroeck | 01 Mar 2016
"

A big focus of many companies is on preventing attacks, which makes sense. If you properly protect your assets and thus prevent all attacks, there’s no reason to look at other approaches. Right? There’s only one small issue with this approach: it is impossible to prevent all attacks. No matter the amount of money you spend or the amount of experts you bring in, sooner or later every company will be successfully attacked. The question is when, not if.

Most companies mainly focus on preventing attacks. For them, the ultimate test (or more commonly, the last check in the box) is the execution of a penetration test on the target in question, and the follow-up of the risks identified. Usually, this is where it ends, which is a big problem. Executing a security assessment provides confidence, but its real value should not be overestimated. Were all possible attacks thoroughly tested? Were no changes applied after the test? And is the version that was tested really an exact copy of what is deployed in production? Companies should understand that a successful attack will occur one day, and should start preparing the next steps.

The first ‘next step’ to get right is the detection of a successful attack, or the actual intrusion. Knowing that an attacker is getting somewhere is of uttermost importance: the sooner you detect this, the sooner you can stop the attacker / start responding. Obviously this step is harder than deploying an IDS; try listing all the protection measures you took, the attacks any network appliance could detect, and make an overview of all the gaps. Yes, there are probably many.

The more mature a company gets, the more the focus should shift away from the prevention side (which should require little additional effort) to the incident preparedness side. Are you, and the people responsible, prepared to deal with the most common / most impactful attacks? Are you also well protected when your country faces its biggest sports challenge and everyone’s out to watch? Did you provide sufficient resiliency for every link in the chain? And have you thought about that rather unlikely but extremely impactful attack?
