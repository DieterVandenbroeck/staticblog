What to do when passwords die

Dieter Vandenbroeck | 22 Nov 2013
"

Passwords are dead according to Heather Adkins (Google's manager of information security) and Our passwords are failing us according to Michael Barrett (PayPal’s Chief Security Officer). And they’re more than correct: not only do most people use passwords wrong (using a short and easy-to-remember password, preferably the same one for all applications), password policies are enforcing people to use passwords they can’t even remember properly. But what are the alternatives? What will we do when passwords finally die?

Below 3 categories of easy and ready-to-use solutions are outlined. And –to show the creativity of some people- some ‘interesting’ ideas are included as well.

The easiest short-term solution (although no alternative for passwords) is a good password policy: it won't fix all problems that passwords bring us, but it can drastically increase the security without doing a lot of work. The 'best practice' recommendation usually looks something like this: at least 8 characters, including numbers, capital letters, special characters, not based on dictionary words, not equal to the previous x passwords, etc. Let's be honest now, who can remember multiple random passwords with these characteristics? To remember something like that, a structure in the used password is almost required (e.g. my name / the website with capitals + year + some weird character). This increases the search space of your password (26 letters + 26 capital letters + 10 numbers + some special characters) -good!- but introduces patterns for your own convenience -bad!-. It's clear that enforcing a good password policy (strong on security andusable by humans) is the key (why don't you just use an entire sentence as a password?).

Everyone is always pointing towards biometrics as the alternative to passwords. Some of the unique identifiers that are looked at are already well known and widely used (think of your laptop’s fingerprint scanner or facial recognition in Android), while others are rapidly gaining the public's attention (using your heartbeat or iris scan). The main problems with biometrics are uniqueness/aliveness and availability: people can wear makeup or even use pictures to evade facial recognition, and gummy fingers created from a print you left somewhere/everywhere is nothing new. And if the expected reward is big enough, let’s say you don't want the crooks to ‘steal’ your authentication mechanism.

The last category is a broad one, containing all other proposed mechanisms. There are some good and known mechanisms (2-factor authentication using a Yubikey, your RSA- or smartphone token, in-app authentication (Twitter), scanning a QR code, challenge-response systems using a card), and there’s (seemingly) science-fiction alternatives (having an electronic tattoo on your arm, swallowing a pill every day).

In the end, it’s not hard to have your authentication not (solely) depending on ‘just some’ password. But even now, despite so many alternatives being available, only some bigger companies are really trying to get away from this ancient method.
