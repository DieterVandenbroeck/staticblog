One security standard to rule them all?

Dieter Vandenbroeck | 26 Mar 2015
"

There are numerous standards available to guide an organization to secure its (most valuable) assets, each of them with a certain focus and differentiating approach. Today, I present the Information Security Management Maturity Model (ISM3) standard. Although it’s not entirely clear when the first version was published, the review of v1.2 took place in 2006, hinting that the authors had lots of time for the creation of the current version (v2.0). But, let’s address the most important question first: was there a need for another standard, and how does it differentiate?

The starting statement on the official website is encouraging:

If you find that the traditional security concepts of confidentiality, integrity, and availability are sound, don't read any further. If you think that these concepts have shortcomings, and you are looking for an alternative, check our Security Fundamentals series of presentations.

So, the authors have looked at ISO 9001, COBIT, ITIL, ISO 27001, and risk analysis standards like OCTAVE, MEHARI, EBIOS or MAGERIT, and concluded that an approach based on the triad of information security is flawed. This is exciting: it doesn’t really matter if you’re the biggest fan of the ‘default’ approach that is described in all textbooks or not. A critical voice must, if the criticism is based on real information / experience, be heard and could only benefit all groups.

Another interesting point detailed on the website are the focus points of the standard: they integrate maturity levels (a small budget can result in medium maturity), a process-oriented approach (instead of a control-oriented approach), and metrics (because you can’t defend your work if you can’t measure the benefits). Oh, and compatibility with other standards and their certification is possible (although a one-on-one relationship does not necessarily exist). Let’s be honest; this sounds incredibly interesting, but it raises questions on why most people don’t know this ‘revolutionary’ standard. The answer is rather simple: the promises available on the website (and present in the presentations) are not translated into the standard itself.

Just to be clear, my goal is to get people’s interest and attention, even though I’m not linked to the standard in any way. I was informed that, although The Open Group is behind the standard, the standard saw weak participation during the creation and review phases. Anyone interested is encouraged to participate where possible, in order to create something that could benefit many organizations.

To be brutally honest, the quality of the standard is not at a point where you actually trust the ideas and content. Most standards have many industry specialists working months to apply updates, followed by rigorous reviews and discussions on changes. You won’t (easily) find spelling errors, you’ll find consistency across the document, and –it sounds unfair, but it’s true- you won’t be thrown back in time remembering the ‘good’ times when word-art dominated documents.

Sadly, there is yet another problem: the standard is confusing. For every process defined, there is a table listing all important information, including a description, rationale, documentation, input and outputs (still pretty clear until here), activity, scope, update, availability, responsibilities, related processes, and related methodologies (which can be as broad as ‘NSA’).

I truly believe the idea behind the standard is sound, and professionals that have experience with related standards could actually use it to some extent. However, only time will tell if a sufficient amount of people will contribute to create a next version that can stand proudly next to other industry standards, or if a lack of contribution will result in a silent death a couple of years from now.
