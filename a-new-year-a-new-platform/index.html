A new year, a new platform

Dieter Vandenbroeck | 04 Jan 2019
"

With the start of 2019, I also launched the new platform that supports this blog. When I moved away from Blogger early 2017, I searched for something that was easy to manage, straightforward yet customizable, and still offered a good cost-benefit outcome. After looking at multiple options at the time, I settled for a lightweight Amazon instance running a modified AnchorCMS installation.

Even though I put a lot of time in the initial setup of that combination, maintenance took more time than I wanted and I had to spend a lot of additional time to really get the solution that I had in mind (spoiler: I never completed that task). As a result, the time I wanted to spend on this blog were spent in the CMS code instead of on the actual content. And that explains the lack of new posts.

So, a fresh start in 2019. In the end, my search was towards the exact same thing: something simple and easy to manage yet powerful (this time with everything I wanted already included). Yet again I was looking for a SaaS solution that took care of most of the work and I was drawn towards the Ghost platform. The people there are doing an amazing job creating an awesome platform, and everything indicates they are only improving it with time. But exactly the same as last time, their pricing ($29 / month for the basic option) pushed me away. Because there doesn't seem to be another good SaaS provider, I settled once again on managing my own infrastructure and platform. This time I settled for DigitalOcean (lower cost, and more than enough if you don't need fancy whistles and bells on a very large cloud platform) with its one-click installation Ghost droplet. This lets me focus almost entirely on the actual content while retaining the option to dive into the code; the few hands-on actions that remain with me (such as managing the installation, certificate, etc.) are few enough.

In the end, most of the time was spent on moving the content. Dumping the old database into the new database would have been possible. This would only take care of the actually existing content, without the associated meta data (which wasn't well formatted to be honest) if it existed at all. A migration like this is the perfect time to make sure all required (meta) data is present and consistent, so the migration (including the enrichment) needed to be done manually. In retrospect, this time I'm a lot better prepared in case another migration would be needed!

All in all, I spent a good amount of time on this, but learned about CAA (Certificate Authority Authorization) in the meantime and finally got to test out some cool tools (including the free Qualys vulnerability scanner and some online web scanners). More on this may follow soon...
