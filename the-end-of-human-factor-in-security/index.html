The end of the ‘human factor’ in security services

Dieter Vandenbroeck | 10 Jul 2015
"

It’s not a secret that a lot of jobs will be taken over in the (near) future by computers and robots. According to the Telegraph, about ten million British jobs could be at stake in the next twenty years; that’s about one in three jobs. With repetitive jobs diminishing, other jobs will arise.

One of the jobs that’s rather unlikely to be taken over by computers is related to security services. It’s hard to imagine that a computer can take over a job that requires skills, knowledge, insights and other traits we think we have. If it’s up to DARPA, computers will play a much bigger role in this domain in the future. Will this cost jobs, or will it only help us doing a better job?

It’s no secret that securing a network and all information on it is hard. Apart from legacy systems and multiple vendors, it’s a challenge to get the required budget and to keep up with everything that’s happening. And that’s a limited overview of problems where the weakest link, the end-user, is not even included in the picture.

On average, it takes about one year for defenders to discover a security vulnerability. Even if the vulnerability is publicly known, it takes one year on average. For some people and companies, this will sound like a joke. But think about all of the credit card thefts of the last few years (and the Trump Hotel Collection that confirmed a breach only two days ago). For a lot of companies, one year is probably impossible to achieve in the next few years.

And to make it worse, it’s not an even battle; in defense, all vulnerabilities need to be fixed. In offense, you only need the one vulnerability that gives you what you need. So what if we can automate the defensive side, aligned with an automated offensive side that provides information?

DARPA’s Cyber Grand Challenge aims to do just that: automate the entire defense and offense activities. In the finals taking place in August 2016 (during DefCON), the finalists will be competing on the following areas of excellence: autonomous analysis (of computer software), autonomous patching, autonomous vulnerability scanning, autonomous service resiliency and autonomous network defense. The aim is to see the unraveling of a full capture the flag competition in just minutes (instead of hours when humans are competing), indicating if their abovementioned goal is fiction or possible reality.
