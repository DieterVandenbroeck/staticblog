Bug bounty programs: creating heaven for ethical hackers or opening the gates to mischief?

Dieter Vandenbroeck | 13 Jun 2016
"

Developers will introduce bugs and sysadmins will make configuration errors, and that’s normal. Making mistakes is what makes us human, and even the most controlled processes cannot take every possibility into account. In the end, the right people combined with the right processes can rule out 99,9% of vulnerabilities, but the last 0,1% is impossible to prevent.

A good example of this is the big ‘oops’ moment Let’s Encrypt faced two days ago. Due to a bug in an automated mailing system, between 0 and 7618 email addresses of other users were included in the body of the email sent out. The security of their own product / service was never at risk, but this is not the kind of mistake you expect from an organization that builds on trust and is chaired by representatives of Mozilla, Akamai, universities, Cisco, the Electronic Frontier Foundation, CoreOS and OVH. Of course these people realize what a big mistake this was and of course they check the products in use. But still this kind of mistakes is inevitable…

Bug bounty programs can partially help discovering such bugs. When a company has done its homework, it can open up to the goodwill of the community and (financially) reward hackers that discover and report vulnerabilities instead of abusing them. The organization becomes aware of undetected risks in a cost-effective way (given that it actually tests its products / infrastructure well) and hackers are motivated to follow the guidelines by a cash incentive. Sounds like a win-win situation.

But do you know the reward’s tipping point? Or to put it differently: do you know how big the reward should be for people to submit to your bug bounty program instead of using the discovery for their own good? Do you believe $100 is sufficient for a remote code execution vulnerability in your core infrastructure, or do you think $10,000 would still make the discovery worth selling underground?

The ‘Hack the Pentagon’ program attracted more than 1,400 hackers who were willing to hand over discoveries for cash. A chance to go wild on the Pentagon’s infrastructure without risking prosecution and earning up to $15,000 for discoveries does sound great. But is it enough? What if someone uses the program as reconnaissance without risking detection and can link vulnerabilities together to something truly interesting; would the cash reward be sufficient? Perhaps, but it might not suffice. Let’s just hope the DoD was monitoring all events even closer…
